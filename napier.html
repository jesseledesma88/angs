Is this Autistic Burnout? Why "Just Rest" or "Focus on the Positive" Often Misses the Point

Dr Annabel Avraham
• October 30, 2024 • 5 min read

Relevant for: Everyone

Autistic burnout is one of those experiences that can be easy to miss from the outside and very hard to live through from the inside.

A person experiencing autistic burnout may look withdrawn, flat, “unmotivated,” irritable, anxious, or avoidant. They may stop replying to messages, cancel plans, struggle to manage their time at work, or become much less able to tolerate noise, social demands, changes of plans, food, or making decisions — big or small.

People may then ask: “Are you depressed?” “Are you stressed?” “Are you just exhausted?” “Do you need a better routine?”

Sometimes the answer is yes. Depression, anxiety, trauma, ADHD overwhelm, chronic stress, and work burnout may all be part of the picture. But if autistic burnout may be involved, sometimes the better question to ask is: has this person been living for too long in a way that does not fit their nervous system?

What is autistic burnout?

Autistic burnout is a state of deep, ongoing exhaustion linked to the cumulative pressure of living in a world that is often not designed for autistic needs.

It is not just feeling tired after a busy week or poor sleep. It can feel like previously available capacities have gone offline. Speaking, planning, working, masking, socialising, parenting, shopping, cooking, showering, or replying to messages may suddenly require impossible effort. The internal means are just not accessible.

This is not laziness. It is not weakness. It is not a personality flaw, or lack of consideration or care. It is a system running beyond capacity for too long.

Autistic burnout can look like depression - but it's not

Both may involve withdrawal, low energy, and a sense of “I can’t do life right now.”

But depression often brings a heavier change in mood, hope, pleasure, self-worth, and the ability to look forward to certain events. It often stays consistent while life demands change and sensory input fluctuates.

A person entering an autistic burnout phase may still feel curiosity, humour, interest, or connection when demands are reduced and the environment becomes safer. They may come alive around a trusted person, a special interest, a familiar routine, or a low-demand space.
